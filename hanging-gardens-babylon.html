Hanging Gardens of Babylon

Iraq

Ancient Wonder

Overview

The Hanging Gardens of Babylon are legendary terraced gardens said to have been located in ancient Babylon, Iraq. They are one of the most enigmatic of the Seven Wonders, as no definitive archaeological evidence has been found to prove their existence.

Historical Accounts

According to ancient sources, the gardens were built by King Nebuchadnezzar II (605-562 BCE) for his Median wife Amytis, who missed her mountainous homeland. The gardens were described by Greek historians Strabo and Diodorus Siculus as extraordinarily beautiful and ingeniously constructed.

Description

Ancient texts describe the Hanging Gardens as a series of vaulted arches built of baked brick and bitumen, rising to a height of about 23 meters (75 feet). The gardens featured exotic plants, trees, and flowers arranged on multiple levels, creating a lush paradise in the midst of the Mesopotamian desert.

Irrigation System

The most remarkable aspect of the gardens was their ingenious irrigation system. According to historical accounts, water was lifted from the Euphrates River using a system of pumps or screw pumps and distributed throughout the gardens to keep the plants alive in the arid climate.

Archaeological Mystery

Despite extensive archaeological excavations in Iraq, no conclusive evidence of the Hanging Gardens has been found. Some scholars suggest they may have been destroyed by earthquakes or floods, while others question whether they ever existed as described in ancient texts. The gardens remain one of history's greatest mysteries.

Cultural Impact

The Hanging Gardens have inspired countless artistic representations, literature, and gardens throughout history. They symbolize the romance of lost civilizations and remain a source of wonder and inspiration to this day.
