Great Pyramid of Giza

Egypt

Ancient Wonder

Overview

The Great Pyramid of Giza is the oldest and only surviving wonder of the Ancient Seven Wonders of the World. Located on the Giza Plateau near Cairo, Egypt, it stands as a testament to the architectural and engineering prowess of ancient civilizations.

Historical Significance

Built during the reign of Pharaoh Khufu (circa 2589-2566 BCE) of the Fourth Dynasty, the Great Pyramid was constructed as an elaborate tomb. It was the tallest man-made structure for over 3,800 years until the completion of the Lincoln Cathedral in 1311 CE.

Construction Details

The pyramid was built using approximately 2.3 million blocks of limestone, each weighing between 2.5 to 15 tons. The construction took approximately 20 years and required thousands of workers. The precision of its construction is remarkable, with the base level to within just 2.1 centimeters.

Dimensions

  • Original Height: 146.5 meters (481 feet)
  • Current Height: 138.5 meters (454 feet)
  • Base Length: 230.4 meters (755 feet) per side
  • Volume: Approximately 2.6 million cubic meters

Architectural Features

The pyramid features a complex internal structure with the King's Chamber, Queen's Chamber, and various passages and shafts. The precision of the angles and orientation is extraordinary—the pyramid is aligned almost perfectly to the cardinal directions.

Modern Significance

Today, the Great Pyramid of Giza remains one of the most visited monuments in the world. It attracts millions of tourists annually and continues to be a subject of archaeological and scientific study. The pyramid symbolizes the ingenuity and ambition of ancient Egypt.
