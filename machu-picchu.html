Machu Picchu

Peru

Modern Wonder

Overview

Machu Picchu is an ancient Incan citadel located high in the Andes Mountains of Peru. Built in the 15th century, it is one of the most recognizable archaeological sites in the world and a masterpiece of Incan engineering.

Historical Significance

Constructed around 1450 during the reign of Inca Pachacuti, Machu Picchu was likely built as an estate for the Inca emperor or an important religious site. The site was abandoned around the time of the Spanish conquest but remained unknown to the outside world until 1911.

Location and Elevation

Perched at 2,430 meters (7,970 feet) above sea level on a mountain ridge above the Urubamba River valley, Machu Picchu's remote location has preserved it remarkably well. The site covers approximately 13 square kilometers and contains thousands of structures.

Architectural Marvels

Machu Picchu showcases extraordinary Incan stonework without the use of mortar. The structures include temples, residences, storage structures, and watchtowers, all precisely fitted together. The agricultural terraces demonstrate advanced knowledge of farming in mountainous terrain.

Mysteries and Theories

The exact purpose of Machu Picchu remains somewhat mysterious. Theories suggest it was a royal retreat, a religious sanctuary, an astronomical observatory, or a combination of these purposes. The site continues to fascinate archaeologists and historians.

UNESCO World Heritage Site

Machu Picchu was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1983 and was named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World in 2007. It attracts approximately 1 million visitors annually and remains one of Peru's greatest treasures.
