About the Solar System

The Solar System is a vast and complex collection of celestial objects bound together by the gravitational pull of the Sun, a massive star at its center. It includes eight major planets, their moons, dwarf planets like Pluto, and countless smaller bodies such as asteroids, comets, and meteoroids. These objects vary greatly in size, composition, and behavior, offering insights into the formation and evolution of planetary systems.

From the rocky inner planets — Mercury, Venus, Earth, and Mars — to the gas and ice giants — Jupiter, Saturn, Uranus, and Neptune — each world presents unique characteristics shaped by billions of years of cosmic processes. Surrounding them is the Kuiper Belt, home to icy bodies and dwarf planets, and further out lies the distant Oort Cloud, a theoretical sphere of ancient comets.

INNER PLANETS

The Swift Planet

Mercury

Location: Giza, Egypt

Built: c. 2589–2566 BCE

Built For: Pharaoh Khufu

Original Height: 146.5 meters

Known For: The only surviving wonder of the ancient world constructed with 2.3 million limestone blocks.

Built as an elaborate tomb for Pharaoh Khufu of the Fourth Dynasty, the Great Pyramid represents one of humanity's greatest architectural achievements. Originally standing at 146.5 meters tall and covered in smooth white limestone, it reflects the remarkable precision and engineering capabilities of ancient Egypt. It remains the only surviving wonder of the ancient world.

Archaeologists continue to study its internal chambers and construction methods; the precise alignment and massive transport of stone blocks remain subjects of research and admiration. Visitors today can explore nearby funerary complexes and view the pyramid from several vantage points along the Giza plateau.

Today the Great Pyramid continues to inspire research into ancient logistics, labor organization, and astronomical alignments. Conservationists work to balance public access with preservation, managing tourism impact while using non-invasive techniques to probe the monument's hidden spaces. Its cultural resonance endures in literature, archaeology, and popular imagination as a touchstone of human ambition.

The Evening Star

Venus

Location: Babylon, Iraq

Built: c. 610–562 BCE

Built By: King Nebuchadnezzar II

Status: Legendary; no conclusive archaeological evidence

Known For: Terraced gardens with exotic plants and innovative irrigation systems.

The Hanging Gardens of Babylon are renowned in ancient literature as a marvel of engineering and horticulture. According to legend, they were built by King Nebuchadnezzar II to comfort his wife, Amytis, who missed the green hills of her homeland. The gardens featured multiple terraced levels filled with exotic plants, connected by vaulted arches.

Because physical evidence is scarce, historians debate whether the gardens were located in Babylon or were a conflation of several royal gardens in Mesopotamia and Assyria. Descriptions emphasize sophisticated irrigation and terracing techniques that would have been remarkable for the period.

Modern scholarship treats the Hanging Gardens as a combination of textual memory and possible garden-building traditions—an emblem of how landscape architecture could signify political power and imperial prestige. The debate over their historicity highlights how ancient texts, archaeology, and environmental studies must be combined to reconstruct vanished landscapes.

The Blue Planet

Earth

Location: Ephesus, Turkey

Built: c. 550 BCE

Architect: Chersiphron and Metagenes

Dimensions: 115 meters long, 55 meters wide

Known For: Magnificent Ionic temple with 127 marble columns and sculptural decorations.

The Temple of Artemis in Ephesus was one of the largest structures of the ancient world. Dedicated to the Greek goddess of the hunt, this magnificent temple featured 127 marble columns arranged in a double row. The temple was renowned for its intricate sculptures and artistic decorations.

Repeatedly rebuilt after fire and destruction, the temple became a major pilgrimage and artistic center, attracting sculptors whose work influenced Classical art. Only fragments of its foundations and scattered sculptures remain at the archaeological site today.

The Temple of Artemis influenced the development of monumental Greek architecture and sculptural programs, shaping ideas of sacred space and civic display. Even in ruin, the temple's legacy endures through surviving sculptures and written descriptions that inform reconstructions and museum exhibits worldwide.

The Red Planet

Mars

Location: Olympia, Greece

Created: c. 435 BCE

Sculptor: Phidias

Height: 12 meters (40 feet)

Materials: Ivory, gold, ebony, and precious stones

The Statue of Zeus at Olympia was a monumental chryselephantine (gold and ivory) sculpture created by the renowned sculptor Phidias around 435 BCE. Standing approximately 12 meters tall, the statue was one of the most celebrated works of art in the ancient world. It depicted the king of the gods seated on a magnificent throne adorned with jewels and carvings.

The statue's materials and size made it a symbol of wealth and religious devotion; it was housed in the Temple of Zeus and complemented the Olympic sanctuary's cultural importance. The original was lost in late antiquity, but many literary descriptions and small-scale reproductions preserve its visual legacy.

Although the original is lost, its fame influenced Hellenistic monumental sculpture and the conception of divine imagery in public sanctuaries. Artistic copies and literary accounts preserved aspects of its appearance and scale, offering modern historians clues about the aesthetics and religious meanings of large-scale cult statues.
