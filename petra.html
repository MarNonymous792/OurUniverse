Petra

Jordan

Modern Wonder

Overview

Petra is an ancient city carved directly from rose-red sandstone cliffs in southern Jordan. Often referred to as the "Rose City" due to the color of its stone, Petra is one of the most spectacular archaeological sites in the world.

Historical Background

Petra was carved by the Nabateans around 300 BCE as a major trading hub connecting Arabia, the Mediterranean, India, and Egypt. The city served as a commercial center for hundreds of years before being conquered by Romans in 106 CE. It was later abandoned and remained hidden until its rediscovery in 1812.

The Treasury (Al-Khazneh)

The most iconic structure at Petra is the Treasury (Al-Khazneh), a massive facade carved into the rock face standing approximately 40 meters (130 feet) tall. Its intricate Nabatean architecture features elaborate decorations and demonstrates the sophistication of ancient artisans.

Architectural Features

Petra contains numerous carved structures including temples, tombs, altars, and residences. The Monastery (Al-Deir) is another impressive structure, standing 40 meters (130 feet) high. The entire city was carved using simple tools and techniques, showcasing remarkable craftsmanship.

Urban Planning

Petra demonstrates sophisticated urban planning with streets, water channels, and drainage systems carefully integrated into the natural landscape. The city featured public baths, markets, and residential areas, indicating a well-organized settlement.

UNESCO World Heritage Site

Petra was designated a UNESCO World Heritage Site in 1985 and named one of the New Seven Wonders of the World in 2007. Today, it is one of the most visited tourist attractions in the Middle East, attracting hundreds of thousands of visitors annually.
